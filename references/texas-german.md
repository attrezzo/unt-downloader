# Texas German (Texasdeutsch) — Reference for OCR Correction

Texas German was a distinct dialect spoken by German-Texan communities from the 1840s through the mid-20th century. When correcting OCR of Texas German newspapers, do NOT normalize these features to standard Hochdeutsch. They are authentic dialect, not errors.

## Core Principle

**If the OCR reads something that is only 1-2 characters different from a plausible Texas German word, prefer the Texas German reading over a standard German "correction."**

Texas German newspapers freely mixed English and German. This is not sloppy writing — it's the living language of the community. Preserve it.

## 3. Period Spellings (Pre-Reform, NOT Errors)

German underwent major spelling reforms in 1901 and 1996. An 1891 newspaper uses pre-1901 orthography throughout. These are correct for the period:

| 1891 spelling | Modern spelling | Notes |
|--------------|----------------|-------|
| thun | tun | Pre-1901 |
| Theil | Teil | Pre-1901 |
| Thür | Tür | Pre-1901 |
| Thier | Tier | Pre-1901 |
| Noth | Not | Pre-1901 |
| Eigenthum | Eigentum | Pre-1901 |
| Kenntniß | Kenntnis | Pre-1901 ß→ss rule |
| daß / dasz | dass | Period spelling |
| muß | muss | Period ß usage |
| taxiren | taxieren | Period verb ending |
| stationirt | stationiert | Period verb ending |
| concurriren | konkurrieren | Period spelling + verb ending |
| Miethwohnungen | Mietwohnungen | Pre-1901 |
| gänzlich | gänzlich | (same, but OCR often drops terminal h) |

| Photographie | Fotografie | Pre-1901 |
| Bureau | Büro | French spelling standard in period |
| Comité / Komittee | Komitee | Period spellings |
| Adresse | Adresse | (unchanged) |
| -iren | -ieren | Period verb infinitive ending |
| -irt | -iert | Period past participle ending |
| -irung | -ierung | Period nominalization ending |

### Period Punctuation & Typography
- **=** at line end means hyphenation (word continues next line)
- **»** and **«** are quotation marks (Guillemets), standard in period German
- **ß** is used where modern German uses **ss** in many positions
- Compound words may be hyphenated where modern German joins them
- Abbreviations: "u." = "und", "d." = "der/die/das", "z.B." = "zum Beispiel"

## 5. Identifying Texas German vs. OCR Error

**Decision tree when a word looks wrong:**

1. Is it a known Fraktur OCR swap? (Check `fraktur-errors.md`) → Apply correction
2. After applying standard corrections, does the result make sense as Texas German? → Keep it
3. Is it 1-2 characters off from a plausible English loanword used in German? → Use the loanword
4. Is it a period spelling that looks wrong by modern standards? → Keep the period form
5. Does it look like a proper noun (person, place, business)? → Keep as-is, it may be a local name
6. None of the above → Mark as uncertain and tag with metadata

## 6. Common Article Types in Texas German Newspapers

Understanding the genre helps predict vocabulary:

| Section | German header | Typical vocabulary |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| International news | Ausland / Deutschland / Frankreich | Standard Hochdeutsch, diplomatic/military terms |
| Texas news | Inland / Texas | Heavy Texas German, English loanwords, local names |
| Local news | [City name] | Heaviest dialect, most English mixing |
| Festival programs | Programm / Fest / Feier | Turnverein vocabulary, community terms |
| Advertisements | Anzeigen | Product names, prices in $, business English |
| Editorials | (untitled) | More formal German, less English mixing |

**Key insight:** International news sections use more standard German because they're often translated from wire services. Texas/local sections are where authentic Texas German appears most heavily. Weight your dialect awareness accordingly.
